My Masters Project

During my Masters Thesis in Biomedical Engineering at McGill University, under the supervision of Dr. Christine Tardif, I designed, developed, and implemented an MRI Acquisition Sequence, and reconstructed the image. Check out my complete masters thesis here: My Masters Thesis.

But here’s a basic run-down for the casual reader:

MRI acquisition is performed in the frequency domain, not the spatial domain! Which means instead of measuring the signal emitted by one point in space, you acquire the combined signal for the entire object being imaged at one frequency. The frequency is encoded with a spatially varying magnetic field, and in-order to acquire a complete image, many different points in the frequency domain (aka k-space) must be acquired. But how you traverse the points is important too! Usually an evenly spaced grid of points is acquired, called Cartesian sampling.

Here's the trajectory I designed!

Some MRI applications, like quantitative brain mapping, require many different consecutive images of the brain to be acquired. This is very slow, and subjects often move, leading to motion artifacts in the image! To speed up the acquisition, we can traverse the frequency domain in a non-cartesian trajectory. Motion artifacts can be mitigated by oversampling the center of k-space, due to signal averaging.

In-order to rapidly capture consecutive images, there needs to be little delay between the acquired data contributing to the subsequent and preceding images. With these things in mind, I developed a Sieffert-spiral trajectory, which spirals from the edge of k-space, to the center, and returns back to the edge, where it precedes to capture the subsequent image’s data. This picture shows a single interleave of my novel trajectory. In this case, each of the 4 colors contributes to one image, but the number of images captured is flexible.

This acquisition was implemented into a 7T MRI, but the acquired data needed to be inverse Fourier transformed into the spatial domain so the image can be seen. Under cartesian sampling conditions, a simple fast Fourier transform is used, but my 3D non-cartesian trajectory required more complex image reconstruction.

After exploring many different algorithms, one, called Partial-Dual reconstruction with L1-wavelet regularization, worked the best! This was outlined in this paper: Image reconstruction algorithm used but needed to be re-written on python to work for my dataset, which can be found on my github: my github. In the spirit of open science, the entire development and reconstruction of my trajectory can be found on github.

My Youtube Channel

As a side project, I started a YouTube channel where I teach machine learning concepts in a slow, calm, and relaxed manner. I believe that the best way to learn is to take your time, and take it slowly, as to not overwhelm yourself with the complexity of the subject. I also believe that the best way to learn is to teach, so by creating these videos, I am also learning the concepts myself. Check it out here: my Youtube Channel.

I cover a wide range of topics, from the basics of machine learning to more advanced concepts. I also provide practical examples and exercises to help you understand the concepts better. If you're interested in learning more about machine learning, be sure to check out my channel!
